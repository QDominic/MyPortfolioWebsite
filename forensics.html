How does digital forensics help solve crimes?

Most individuals are aware of forensic science, whereby evidence is collected physically from the crime scene to provide information on what took place. This is different from digital forensics, which is not as well-known and refers to a form of investigation where the ‘crime scene’ exists in computerized devices. Due to the global nature of computerized devices, there is need for professionals in this field in criminal investigations.

What is digital forensics used for?

These professionals are involved in the investigation when it is believed that the computer belonging to the criminal or victim and the smartphone, along with embedded systems of other electronic devices, have evidence relating to the whereabouts, communications, web searching, and app usage of the person in question. Such evidence can be used in both physical and cybercrime investigations. For instance, a digital forensic investigator can use the GPS system of the suspect's phone to know his whereabouts. He may examine the documents and coding within the computer system of a hacker to determine security vulnerabilities and theft of information. In cybercrimes, the investigations begin on the computer or the network of the victim, and forensic specialists will be able to discover the source and nature of the crime. The hackers will leave a signature trail during their attacks. This trail will become the key piece of evidence or direct the investigators to damning documents and devices.

Why is digital forensics important?

Cyber-crime cases occur very frequently. In 2024, 859,532 complaints regarding cyber-crimes have been reported to the FBI and the total losses involved were worth over $16 billion, which is 33% higher compared to the previous year. Evidence of these crimes could be found in computers, hard drives, and mobile phones. However, it is not only enough for agencies to seize those devices; the need arises for forensic examiners who will retrieve data from them in order to build up their case against the accused. In most cases, data resides on cloud-based storage services.
